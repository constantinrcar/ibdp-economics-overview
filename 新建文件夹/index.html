IBDP Economics Overview

Market Demand

Definition: Market demand refers to the total quantity of a good or service that all consumers in the market are willing and able to purchase at various price levels.

Key Points: Market demand curve is derived by horizontally summing individual demand curves. Price and non-price factors influence market demand.

Common Mistakes: Confusing market demand with individual demand.

Income Effect (HL)

Definition: Income effect refers to the change in the quantity demanded of a good when a change in price affects consumer's real purchasing power.

Key Points: Price drop increases real income and demand for normal goods but might decrease demand for inferior goods.

Common Mistakes: Confusing income effect with price effect.

Real Income

Definition: Real income refers to the purchasing power of a consumer's nominal income after adjusting for price changes.

Key Points: Real income is impacted by inflation. Higher prices lower real income, reducing purchasing power.

Common Mistakes: Confusing nominal income with real income.

Substitution Effect (HL)

Definition: Substitution effect refers to the tendency of consumers to switch to cheaper alternatives as the price of a good increases.

Key Points: Price changes influence consumer choices between substitutes, and the effect is stronger when more substitutes are available.

Common Mistakes: Mixing substitution effect with income effect.

Marginal Utility (HL)

Definition: Marginal utility refers to the additional satisfaction or utility gained from consuming one more unit of a good or service.

Key Points: Marginal utility typically decreases as more units are consumed, explaining why demand curves slope downward.

Common Mistakes: Confusing marginal utility with total utility.

Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility (HL)

Definition: The law states that as a person consumes more units of a good, the additional utility gained from each extra unit decreases.

Key Points: Explains downward-sloping demand curves. As more of a good is consumed, willingness to pay decreases.

Common Mistakes: Thinking that diminishing marginal utility leads to decreasing total utility, which is not necessarily true.
